3H 1 Soil pH and acid rain

The pH (acidity) of soil is important. Most plants will grow well only in soil with the correct pH. It isn’t the H+ ions that matter to the plants – it is the correct concentration of metal ions such as iron, manganese and aluminium. In neutral or alkaline (basic) soils, these ions are present in the form of insoluble hydroxides, unavailable to the plants. As the acidity of the soil increases, the hydroxides dissolve, releasing the metal ions. Too much acid will release too many metal ions, poisoning the plants. Natural soils are slightly acidic. Most fertilisers are also acidic (superphosphate has a pH of 4) and in some countries acid rain makes soil even more acidic. Farmers and home gardeners usually use crushed limestone (CaCO3) to neutralise the soil. Sometimes they used slaked lime (calcium hydroxide), which is more expensive, but gives a faster result. If magnesium is also needed in the soil they use dolomite, which is about 90% calcium carbonate and 10% magnesium carbonate.

      The concentration increases as acidity increases, because the insoluble hydroxide is converted to the soluble nitrate, making the Fe2+ ions more available.

  2. In many industrialised countries, air pollution causes acid rain. Although people don’t notice the rain’s acidity when they walk through it, and the flowers don’t seem to be affected when acid rain falls on them, acid rain kills thousands of hectares of forests.
    1. Explain how acid rain, even when the acidity is not strong enough for people to taste, can lead to the deaths of plants.

      Acid rain causes insoluble metal hydroxides to be converted to soluble salts. Over time the concentration of these metal ions builds up in the soil and poison the plants.

    2. What can people living in countries affected by acid rain do if they want to grow plants such as tomatoes and sweet peas in their gardens? Explain your answer.

      They can add chemicals such as calcium carbonate or calcium hydroxide to neutralise soil acidity, since these plants require neutral to slightly basic soils.

  3. William’s new house has a strip of garden along the fence. Part of this garden has a concrete path beside it, while the rest is beside grass. He decided to alternate azaleas and roses along this fence. Why would his garden centre advise him to plant all the roses beside the path and put the azaleas beside the grass?

    Concrete contains calcium hydroxide (lime), so the soil beside the concrete path will be more alkaline than the soil beside the grass. It is best to keep the acid-loving azaleas far away from the concrete path by planting them beside the grass. Roses like neutral conditions, but will probably do OK beside the path as long as the concrete isn't too new.
    Recognises that concrete is alkaline.
    Links azaleas preference for acid soil to the need to keep them away from concrete.
