3I 6 Rates questions

  1. State which mixture has the faster reaction rate in each case.
    1. 2 g of magnesium powder in dilute HCl or 2 g of magnesium ribbon in dilute HCl.

      The powder reacts faster than the ribbon.
    2. 2 g of magnesium ribbon in dilute HCl at 20 °C or 2 g of magnesium ribbon in dilute HCl at 40 °C.

      Dilute HCl at 40 °C reacts faster than dilute HCl at 20 °C.
    3. 2 g of magnesium ribbon in concentrated HCl or 2 g of magnesium ribbon in dilute HCl.

      Concentrated HCl reacts faster than dilute HCl.
  2. When a lump of clean zinc reacts with dilute sulfuric acid, does the zinc react faster at the start of the reaction or at the finish? Give two reasons for your answer.

    Faster at the start.
    The lump of zinc gets smaller as the reaction proceeds, which means its surface area decreases which decreases the reaction rate.
    The concentration of the acid also decreases as the reaction proceeds which also decreases the reaction rate.

  3. The graph shows the volume of carbon dioxide given off by the action of dilute hydrochloric acid on calcium carbonate.
    1. What is happening to the reaction rate as time passes?

      The rate of reaction gets slower/decreases.
    2. Why does the graph become horizontal after a certain time?

      The reaction has stopped
      because one of the reagents (the acid or the calcium carbonate) has been used up.

  4. A splinter of wood which was just glowing in air burst into flame when placed in a test tube containing oxygen gas. Use your knowledge of collision theory to discuss why this happens.

    Oxygen molecules only make up about 20% of air, so the number of collisions between oxygen atoms and the glowing splint will be quite low in air. In the test tube of oxygen the concentration of oxygen is very high, and the number of collisions between the splint and oxygen molecules will be much greater, so the reaction rate will be much greater. Each successful collision generates heat which causes the particles to move faster, increasing the number of collisions per second and the proportion of successful collisions. This further increases the reaction rate. Eventually so much heat is released that the splint ignites.

    Recognises the difference in concentration of oxygen in air and in pure oxygen.
    Links high and low concentrations of oxygen to the number of collisions between oxygen molecules and the splint.
    Links number of collisions to reaction rate.
    States that reaction generates heat.
    Recognises that an increased temperature causes particles to move faster.
    Links faster particle movement to increased number of collisions.
    Links faster particle movement to increased number of successful collisions.
